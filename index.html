What Are Exoplanets?

The name may sound a bit scary, but 'Exoplanets' are simply those planets that do not orbit our Sun, hence revolve around other stars and are not a part of our solar system. These alien worlds were first found in the late 20th century, and since then, we have discovered thousands of them.
But why peer through the sky and look for them? We have been noticing the stars out in the sky for millions of years. Why is some other piece of rock and dust, or perhaps gas, significant? Well, observing exoplanets helps us understand how planetary systems form, how they evolve, and whether conditions for life exist elsewhere in the cosmos.
So wait a minute, are we trying to contact aliens? I will leave that to your introspection.

Transiting Exoplanets

Although there are several methods that astronomers have used to find exoplanets ‚Äî radial velocity, gravitational lensing, direct imaging ‚Äî this guide focuses on the commonly known and widely used 'Transit Method'. Since my intentions are not to bore you, I have attached an easy-to-understand, quick video below, giving you a basic idea.

Let‚Äôs Dissect What You Just Saw:
  • The green line, being drawn below the star and the planet orbiting, is called the "light curve."
  • The dip in the light curve that happens when the planet passes in front of the star is called the "transit."
  • The length of time between each transit is the planet's "orbital period," or the length of a year on that particular planet.
  • This orbital period is indicative of the radius of the planet's orbit. If it‚Äôs not too close nor too far from the star, then it might be in the habitable zone. An alien planet?
Different Sized Planets Exhibit Different Light Curves

Measuring the transit depth is not enough to measure the size of the planet -- you also need to know the size of the star it is transiting. The image below illustrates how a planet transiting a small star (on the right) causes a larger transit signal than the same sized planet transiting a large star on the left:

Specifically, the transit depth is equal to the ratio between the planet's radius (Rp) and the star's radius (R*) squared:

\( \text{Depth} = \left( \frac{R_p}{R_\star} \right)^2 \)

Meaning that the planet's radius can be found using the following formula:

\( R_p = R_\star \sqrt{\text{Depth}} \)

Understanding Transit Light Curves

Any fool can know
The point is to understand
Albert Einstein

In this section, we will use an example light curve from the Kepler's Mission to determine planetary parameters.

Launched in 2009, Kepler spent nearly a decade searching for transiting exoplanets before concluding its mission in 2018. During its operation, it stood as the most powerful planet-hunting observatory ever built. Similarly, there's also an ongoing TESS Mission that aims to find exoplanets around nearby stars. Best of all, every bit of data these missions collected is freely available to the public -- ready to be downloaded by anyone with an internet connection.

Shown below is a light curve of an exoplanet called HAT-P-7b

Notice the y-axis. The entire range of brightness is just 1, and the span of the graph stays within that value. It is like zooming in on a small section of the brightness versus time graph (only about 1 percent of the total) to observe a conspicuous dip. This shows that searching for transiting planets demands extremely precise brightness measurements.

The light curve of HAT-P-7b shows three dips, or transits, marked with red arrows. It usually takes at least three observed transits to confirm the existence of a planet, since many other cosmic objects, such as asteroids, can also pass in front of the star being studied.

As mentioned earlier, depth of the transits encodes the size of the planet. The depth, to be specific, tells how much light is blocked. Therefore, bigger planets will block more light exhibiting a deeper transit.

Let's find the size of this planet:
  1. We know the formula:
    \( R_p = R_\star \sqrt{\text{Depth}} \)
    • where, \( R_\star = 2 \) Solar Radii
  2. Since the brightness dips to around 0.994,
    • \( \text{Depth} = 1 - 0.994 = 0.006 \)
  3. Using this we get, \( R_p = 16.8 \) Earth Radii
Therefore, this calculation tells us that HAT-P-7b is roughly 4470 times the volume of Earth.

Lastly, The amount of time between two successive transits is the orbital period (YR) of the planet, which is the length of that planet's year.

\( YR = 3.4 - 1.2 = 2.2 \) Days

So, with an orbital period of just 2.2 days, a year on that planet would be over before your weekend plans even start. And if you were 20 years old on Earth, you‚Äôd be celebrating your 3,300th birthday over there -- and probably very tired of cake.

Zooming into the dip, we find that the transit is round-bottom, rather than exhibiting a flat-bottomed profile. This profile arises because stars are not uniformly bright over their surfaces. This is illustrated in the images below.

Zooming into the first transit inorder to calculate the size of this planet:
  1. We know the formula:
    \( R_p = R_\star \sqrt{\text{Depth}} \)
    • where, \( R_\star = 0.5 \) Solar Radii

      • Avoiding the intricacies of vast catalogs, a simple Google search with star's TIC ID can reveal its radius quickly.

  2. Since the brightness dips to around 0.95 from approx 1,
    • \( \text{Depth} = 1 - 0.95 = 0.05 \)
  3. Using this we get, \( R_p = 0.112 \) Solar Radii or \( R_p = 0.108 \) Jupiter Radii
Therefore, this calculation tells us that this planet is roughly 0.108 times the radius of Jupiter.

Zooming out, we find another transit with approximately the same depth, indicating that its the same planet causing it. Using the time difference between these two, we can easily deduce the orbital period (yr) on the planet.
  1. The time at which the first transit occurs is \( 3434.855 \) and the second transit occurs at \( 3442.028 \)
  2. So, the orbital period (yr) is,
    • \( YR = 3442.028 - 3434.855 \approx 7 \) Days
Therefore, in layman terms, a year on that planet would be just 7 days long.
